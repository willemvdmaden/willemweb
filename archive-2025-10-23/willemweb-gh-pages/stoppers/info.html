Research Information

Background

The Challenge of AI Integration

While generative AI tools offer tremendous potential for design research and practice, their integration into existing workflows is often far from seamless. Researchers and practitioners regularly encounter obstacles that prevent effective adoption, slow down progress, or create new problems that didn't exist before.

These blockers are not just technical issues—they encompass a complex web of technical, social, organizational, and ethical challenges that emerge when new technologies meet established practices.

Three Types of GenAI Barriers

Through our research, we've identified three distinct types of barriers that prevent effective GenAI integration:

1. Semantic Stopsigns

These are the big narratives, catchphrases, or conceptual frameworks that shut down deeper engagement with GenAI before it can begin. Think of statements like "AI will replace human creativity" or "AI is just plagiarism" - these reductive framings provide an illusion of resolution while actually preventing nuanced exploration. They often contain partial truths worth considering, but become problematic when they halt inquiry entirely, leading to either blanket rejection or uncritical acceptance of GenAI technologies.

Subcategories:
  • Political: Institutional policies or power dynamics that prevent GenAI exploration
  • Social: Cultural attitudes or peer pressure that discourage GenAI engagement
  • Conceptual: Oversimplified beliefs about GenAI that shut down deeper thinking

2. Practical Blockers

These are the practical, technical, or implementation challenges that kill momentum when you're actively trying to work with GenAI. Unlike semantic stopsigns that prevent you from starting, practical blockers emerge during hands-on engagement - when the technology doesn't work as expected, creates workflow disruptions, or produces that "dirty" feeling from automating processes that feel inherently human. These include technical limitations, unexpected cognitive load from evaluation tasks, or the gap between GenAI's promise and its messy reality in practice.

Subcategories:
  • Technical Limitations: The technology simply can't do what you need it to do
  • Workflow Disruption: GenAI creates more work or confusion than it solves
  • Skill Gaps: Missing knowledge or training needed to use GenAI effectively
  • Resource Constraints: Lack of time, budget, or computational resources to implement properly

3. Design Tensions

These are situations where GenAI creates genuine conflicts between legitimate design goals, values, or priorities - forcing you to choose between two "good" things. Unlike stopsigns that halt engagement or practical blockers that create practical obstacles, design tensions acknowledge that even when GenAI works well, it can force difficult trade-offs. For example, an AI system might deliver highly personalized experiences but require privacy compromises, or provide rapid iteration capabilities while potentially reducing craft and intentionality. These tensions require navigating competing priorities rather than finding technical solutions.

Subcategories:
  • Experience vs. Control: AI enhances user experience but reduces designer control over outcomes
  • Efficiency vs. Quality: AI speeds up processes but potentially compromises depth or craftsmanship
  • Innovation vs. Tradition: AI enables new possibilities but conflicts with established practices or values
  • Accessibility vs. Authenticity: AI makes things more accessible but may dilute original intent or meaning
  • Personalization vs. Privacy: AI customization requires data trade-offs that feel uncomfortable
  • Automation vs. Agency: AI handles tasks well but removes human agency from meaningful processes

Beyond Simple Solutions

Many discussions about AI adoption focus on success stories or general benefits. However, real-world integration is messy, context-dependent, and filled with unexpected challenges. By systematically documenting and analyzing these curiosity stoppers (inspired by the concept of semantic stopsigns), we can develop more nuanced understanding and practical strategies for overcoming them.

Moving Past Stoppers: Different Approaches

There's no single "right" way to work through GenAI barriers. Here are several approaches that researchers and practitioners have developed:

Theory U Framework (Our Research Approach)

Following Otto Scharmer's change management framework, we:

  • Identify the stopper and how it functions in discourse
  • Suspend judgment to explore alternative dimensions
  • Sense new perspectives and possibilities
  • Crystallize actionable insights for your context

This process helps move from surface reactions to deeper engagement, whether that leads to adoption or informed resistance.

Artistic Inquiry

Some artists and designers use stoppers as creative prompts rather than barriers. Examples:

  • Shahryar Nashat's Reverse Rorschach turns AI unpredictability into artistic dialogue
  • Projects that deliberately explore the "dirty feeling" of automated creativity
  • Using AI "hallucinations" as sources of creative inspiration

Design Constraints

Reframing apparent obstacles as design opportunities:

  • Environmental concerns become sustainability design challenges
  • IP restrictions drive innovation in attribution practices
  • Technical limitations spark creative workarounds

Inclusive Resistance

Deliberately seeking out dissenting voices in AI processes:

  • Including AI skeptics in design teams
  • Balancing evangelist and critic perspectives
  • Using tension as a generative force

Other Approaches?

What methods have you developed? We're always interested in learning from the community.

Research Approach

Data Collection

We're collecting detailed accounts of blocker experiences through:

  • Structured submission forms capturing specific blocker incidents
  • In-depth interviews with researchers and practitioners
  • Analysis of existing literature and case studies
  • Participatory workshops where participants work through blockers together

Analysis Framework

For each blocker, we examine:

  • Context: What was the setting, task, and goal when the blocker occurred?
  • Nature: What exactly prevented progress? Was it technical, social, organizational, or ethical?
  • Impact: How did the blocker affect the work, timeline, or outcomes?
  • Response: What strategies were attempted to overcome the blocker?
  • Resolution: Was the blocker resolved, worked around, or did it persist?
  • Lessons: What insights emerged from the experience?

Collaborative Analysis

This is not just an academic exercise—we're building a community of practice around thoughtful AI integration. Participants in our research contribute their experiences and also engage in collaborative analysis sessions where we work together to identify patterns and develop solutions.

How to Participate

Who Can Participate

We welcome contributions from anyone who has encountered blockers while working with generative AI, including:

  • Design researchers and practitioners
  • UX researchers and designers
  • Software developers and engineers
  • Academics and students
  • Product managers and strategists
  • Artists and creative professionals
  • Educators and trainers

What We're Looking For

We're interested in specific, detailed accounts of blockers you've encountered. This could include:

  • Times when AI tools failed to deliver expected results
  • Workflow disruptions caused by AI integration attempts
  • Ethical or professional concerns that arose during AI use
  • Organizational pushback or resistance to AI adoption
  • Technical challenges in implementing AI solutions
  • Unexpected consequences or side effects of AI use

Privacy and Ethics

We take privacy and ethics seriously in this research:

  • All submissions are handled confidentially
  • You can choose whether to be identified or remain anonymous
  • Organization and project details can be anonymized
  • You control how your contribution is used in publications
  • You can withdraw your participation at any time

Expected Outcomes

For Participants

  • Opportunity to reflect on and learn from your experiences
  • Access to insights and strategies from other practitioners
  • Connection to a community of thoughtful AI adopters
  • Co-authorship opportunities on resulting publications
  • Early access to research findings and frameworks

For the Field

  • Comprehensive taxonomy of AI adoption blockers
  • Evidence-based strategies for overcoming common obstacles
  • Guidelines for organizations implementing AI tools
  • Framework for evaluating AI integration readiness
  • Academic publications and conference presentations
  • Open-access resources and tools

Project Timeline

  • Phase 1 (Current): Collecting blocker experiences through submissions and interviews
  • Phase 2 (Next 3 months): Initial analysis and pattern identification
  • Phase 3 (6 months): Collaborative analysis workshops with participants
  • Phase 4 (9 months): Framework development and validation
  • Phase 5 (12 months): Publication and resource creation

Timeline is approximate and may adjust based on the volume and depth of contributions received.
